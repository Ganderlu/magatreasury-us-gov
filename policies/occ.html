Office of the Comptroller of the Currency (OCC)

This page provides an overview of the Office of the Comptroller of the Currency (OCC) for informational purposes. It explains what the OCC is, what it does, how it interacts with the U.S. banking system, and where to find official resources.

1) What is the OCC?

The Office of the Comptroller of the Currency (OCC) is a U.S. government bureau responsible for regulating and supervising national banks and federal savings associations. The OCC’s work focuses on helping ensure that these institutions operate in a safe and sound manner, provide fair access to financial services, and comply with applicable laws and regulations.

2) What institutions does the OCC supervise?

The OCC generally supervises:

  • National banks (banks chartered under federal law)
  • Federal savings associations
  • Federal branches and agencies of foreign banks (in certain contexts)
Note: Different regulators may supervise other types of financial institutions. The OCC is one part of a broader regulatory ecosystem.

3) What does “safe and sound” mean?

In general, “safe and sound” refers to whether a bank is operating with prudent risk management, adequate capital and liquidity, effective governance, and controls that support stability and resilience. Safety and soundness supervision may include reviewing:

  • Capital levels and risk-weighted exposures
  • Liquidity management and funding practices
  • Credit risk, market risk, and operational risk controls
  • Compliance programs and internal audit effectiveness
  • Third-party risk management (vendors and service providers)
  • Cybersecurity and technology governance

4) Common areas of focus

Consumer protection

Banks are expected to treat customers fairly, provide clear disclosures, and maintain processes that reduce unfair, deceptive, or abusive practices.

Anti‑money laundering (AML)

Banks typically maintain AML programs and compliance measures to detect and help prevent illicit financial activity, consistent with relevant requirements.

Operational resilience

Institutions are expected to manage outages, incidents, and business continuity risks, including technology and third-party dependencies.

Corporate governance

Supervisory expectations often include effective board oversight, clear accountability, and strong internal control environments.

5) OCC examinations and supervision

The OCC conducts supervisory activities and examinations to evaluate a bank’s condition and compliance. This process can include reviewing documents and policies, meeting with management, analyzing risk metrics, and assessing internal controls and governance.

When issues are identified, the OCC may require corrective actions or enhancements. Depending on circumstances, supervisory actions can range from informal guidance to formal enforcement actions.

6) Public information and official resources

For the most accurate and up-to-date information, use official sources. The OCC provides publications, guidance, alerts, and educational resources.

  • Official website: occ.treas.gov
  • Consumer assistance information is typically available through the OCC website’s help and resources sections.
  • Public notices, enforcement actions, and releases may also be accessible from the OCC site.
