Data Transparency

Data transparency helps the public understand how tax policy decisions are made and what impacts are expected. Transparent reporting makes it easier to compare proposals, evaluate results, and improve policy over time.

What “Transparency” Typically Means

  • Clear definitions: consistent terminology and categories so information is comparable.
  • Documented methods: explanation of how estimates are produced, including limitations.
  • Accessible presentation: summaries, tables, and visuals that help people interpret results.
  • Reproducible structure: stable formats and metadata that support analysis.

Common Data and Analysis Artifacts

Examples

  • Distributional summaries showing impacts across income groups
  • Revenue estimates across time horizons (short-term and long-term)
  • Descriptions of assumptions and baseline scenarios
  • Technical appendices explaining model or estimation approach
  • Data dictionaries and definitions for published tables

Why It Matters

Transparent information supports accountability and reduces confusion. It helps stakeholders understand tradeoffs and how a policy may affect households, businesses, and government finances.

  • Improves trust by making assumptions visible.
  • Supports better public debate and feedback.
  • Enables comparisons across proposals and across years.
  • Helps identify unintended impacts earlier.
