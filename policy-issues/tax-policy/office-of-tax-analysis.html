Tax Analysis

Tax analysis helps evaluate how tax rules affect revenue, distribution, economic behavior, and administrative feasibility. Analysis is used to compare options, understand tradeoffs, and improve policy design.

What Analysts Typically Examine

  • Revenue effects: expected changes in receipts over a defined budget window.
  • Distributional impacts: how benefits and burdens are allocated across households and businesses.
  • Behavioral responses: changes in incentives to work, save, invest, or report.
  • Complexity: compliance burden and administrative feasibility.
  • Interactions: how changes affect other provisions and existing programs.

Common Outputs

Examples

  • Revenue and distribution tables
  • Scenario and sensitivity analyses
  • Assumptions and baseline documentation
  • Implementation and compliance risk notes
  • Technical summaries for decision-makers and the public

Using Analysis Responsibly

Good analysis states assumptions clearly and acknowledges uncertainty. Different models can produce different results depending on data, definitions, and behavioral assumptions.

  • Document assumptions and scope.
  • Explain limitations and uncertainty.
  • Use consistent baselines for comparability.
  • Consider administrative realities and compliance burden.
